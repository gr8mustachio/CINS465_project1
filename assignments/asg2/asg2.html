The History of Chess can be followed all the way back to around 1500 years ago. While the origins themselves might be contested, we know for a fact that it originated in South Asia, more specifically India. It spread to the Middle East, then Russia and finally western europe by the 9th century. Chess as we know it today came to be around 1500 CE. There are six different types of chess pieces, and each side begins with 16 pieces The goal is to capture the other player's king, and both sides can capture as many pieces as the other side possess. Chess meta has evolved extensively as the years have gone on, with the current popular strategy being slow paced, claculated games where both players are (hopefully) thinking several steps ahead of the current move. Let's dive into the different types of chess pieces present on a modern day checkerboard.

Meet the Chess Pieces!
  • Pawn
    • The most numerous piece on the board. Each player gets 8 pawns. On their first move, they have the choice of either moving one or two spaces foreward. After this, they cannot move two spaces. They can either move forward one, or it can capture a piece that is diagonally foreward by 1 square to its position. It is the only piece that cannot move backwards.
  • Knight
    • Each player gets two Knights on their side. The knight is a unique piece in the sense that it acually has two different forms of traversal on the board. It can either move up 2 places and over 1, or up 1 place and over 2. The color it lands on must be different than the one it was originally from.
  • Rook
    • Each player gets two Rooks on their side. The rook can move in 4 separate directions, up, down, left, and right, and can do so until it either runs into another friendly piece or captures an enemy piece.
  • Bishop
    • Each player gets two Bishops on their side. The bishop can move in 4 separate directions diagonally, up->left, down->right, up->left, and up->right, and can do so until it either runs into another friendly piece or captures an enemy piece.
  • Queen
    • Each player gets one Queen. It must be used carefully, as it is the most overpowered chess piece, combining the movements of the Rook and the Bishop makes it a formidible piece to be up against, and a big loss to whoever loses their Queen.
  • The King
    • Each player gets one King. It must be guarded carefully, and positioned even more carefully, as it is the most valuble chess piece. It can move one tile in any direction. If a player moves a piece so that it puts the king at risk of being captured the next turn, the other player must "check" so that their king is no longer in danger. If that isnt possible, then the game is at a "checkmate" and the player whose king is in danger loses.
